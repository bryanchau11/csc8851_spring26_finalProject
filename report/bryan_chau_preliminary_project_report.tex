\begin{abstract}
This report presents our final team system for nutrition estimation from a single RGB meal image. The pipeline combines YOLOv8-seg masking, MiDaS depth, a 9D geometric descriptor, direct nutrient regression (phase 4), and weight-first regression with nutrient conversion (phase 6), with MC Dropout for uncertainty. On the 326-dish test split, phase 4 achieves calorie MAE/RMSE 119.79/158.69 and improves calorie MAE by 74.46 over the matched phase-3 baseline; phase 6 obtains weight MAE/RMSE 98.9 g/152.2 g with derived calorie MAE 133.8. The phase-5 EfficientNet-B0 classifier reaches 85.86\% top-1 and 97.03\% top-5, and the integrated system is deployed in Gradio with editable portions and confidence-aware outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Automated food nutrition estimation remains difficult because visual appearance is not a direct proxy for mass or composition. Portion size can change significantly with camera distance and plate arrangement, and dishes with similar color or shape can have very different energy density.

Our final team goal is practical single-image estimation of calories, fat, protein, and carbohydrates with uncertainty reporting. The project repository is:
\begin{center}
\url{https://github.com/bryanchau11/csc8851_spring26_finalProject}
\end{center}

We implemented a multi-phase system with graceful fallback when checkpoints are missing. The final integrated mode (``full'') combines phases 4, 5, and 6 in one app.

\textbf{Main contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item A depth-aware geometric feature extractor from segmentation masks and MiDaS predictions.
\item Two complementary nutrient paths: direct macro regression and weight-first conversion.
\item A food-type classifier used for top-$k$ dish labels and USDA density prior retrieval.
\item End-to-end uncertainty propagation with MC Dropout and user-facing confidence display.
\item A production-style Gradio interface for image upload, nutrient prediction, and ingredient editing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
Prior approaches generally fall into three categories.

\textbf{Image-level food recognition.}
Food-101 and related classification benchmarks motivate transfer learning with CNN backbones such as EfficientNet~\cite{bossard2014food101,tan2019efficientnet}. These methods identify dish category well but do not directly solve portion estimation.

\textbf{Nutrition regression from meal images.}
Nutrition5K-style benchmarks frame calorie/macronutrient prediction from meal photos~\cite{nutrition5kweb}. Direct RGB-to-nutrients models can work, but they may conflate food identity and quantity, reducing robustness under viewpoint changes.

\textbf{Segmentation and geometric cues.}
Modern instance segmentation (YOLOv8) and monocular depth estimators (MiDaS) provide useful structure for volume-related features from one view~\cite{ultralytics2023,midasrepo}. Our method uses these signals to improve amount estimation before nutrient conversion.

\section{Dataset and Experimental Protocol}
\subsection{Data and Targets}
Our nutrition pipeline is built around Nutrition5K-derived records containing meal-level nutrient labels:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}=[\text{calories},\text{fat},\text{protein},\text{carb}].
\label{eq:targets}
\end{equation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:targets} defines the four meal-level targets. The final phase-4 notebook evaluation logs a split of 2597 train / 324 val / 326 test dishes. We report MAE and RMSE as primary metrics and include MAPE only as a secondary signal, because near-zero nutrient labels can inflate percentage errors.

\subsection{Phased Development}
The codebase follows six phases:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Phase 3}: ResNet-50 direct nutrient baseline.
\item \textbf{Phase 4}: geometric-feature MLP direct regression.
\item \textbf{Phase 5}: EfficientNet-B0 food classifier (Food-101).
\item \textbf{Phase 6}: weight-first MLP with nutrient conversion constants.
\end{itemize}
Phases 1--2 focus on data exploration and feature engineering; phase 7 handles optional ingredient segmentation training artifacts.

Training was compute-intensive, so major jobs were executed on Kaggle GPU instances. Classifier/segmentation training and ablation reruns required multi-hour sessions before checkpoint selection, and final weights were exported for local inference in the Gradio app. Figure~\ref{fig:epoch_training} marks where epoch-level training curves should be inserted.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Pipeline Overview}
Given RGB image $I$, we estimate a mask $M$ and depth map $D$. A geometric encoder computes feature vector $g\in\mathbb{R}^9$. Two nutrient paths are then computed:
\begin{align}
\hat{\mathbf{n}} &= f_\theta(g), \label{eq:direct_path}\\
\hat{w} &= h_\phi(g), \qquad \hat{n}_j = \hat{w}\rho_j. \label{eq:weight_path}
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:direct_path} is the direct nutrient regression path, and Eq.~\eqref{eq:weight_path} is the weight-first conversion path with per-gram constants $\rho_j$. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the full system data flow, and Table~\ref{tab:components} summarizes each module used during final inference.

\subsection{Feature and Model Details}
The app implementation in \path{app/core\_models.py} defines the phase-4 and phase-6 MLPs as:
\begin{itemize}
\item Input 9 features
\item Hidden layers: 128 $\rightarrow$ 64 $\rightarrow$ 32
\item BatchNorm + ReLU + Dropout (0.2)
\item Output: 4 nutrients (phase 4) or 1 scalar weight (phase 6)
\end{itemize}

The phase-5 classifier uses EfficientNet-B0 features with a dropout-MLP head for 101 Food-101 classes.

\subsection{Uncertainty Modeling}
At inference, dropout remains active for $T$ stochastic forward passes (typically $T=30$ in app inference and $T=50$ in notebook evaluation). For prediction samples $\{\hat{y}^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^T$:
\begin{align}
\mu &= \frac{1}{T}\sum_t \hat{y}^{(t)}, \label{eq:mc_mean}\\
\sigma^2 &= \frac{1}{T-1}\sum_t\left(\hat{y}^{(t)}-\mu\right)^2. \label{eq:mc_var}
\end{align}
Equations~\eqref{eq:mc_mean} and \eqref{eq:mc_var} are the Monte Carlo mean and sample variance estimates~\cite{gal2016dropout}. We report confidence as approximately $\mu \pm 2\sigma$.

For weight-first macros, uncertainty is propagated linearly:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{n_j}=\rho_j\sigma_w.
\label{eq:propagate}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:propagate} is also used in the app confidence display.

\subsection{App Integration and Fallback Logic}
The runtime mode resolver supports multiple checkpoint combinations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{full}: phase 6 + phase 4 + phase 5 loaded
\item \texttt{phase6}: weight-first only
\item \texttt{phase4}: direct regression only
\item \texttt{phase3}: ResNet fallback
\end{itemize}
When food classification confidence is low, the app avoids overconfident labels via a minimum confidence threshold. If USDA dish-specific constants exist, they override generic constants from the local metadata cache and FoodData Central lookup~\cite{usdafdc}.

\subsection{Deployment Behavior and Human-in-the-Loop Editing}
The deployed Gradio interface supports post-inference correction: users can edit ingredient portions (amount/unit), and the backend recomputes totals using USDA per-food densities when available, otherwise fallback dish-level constants. To improve safety and interpretability, the app exposes intermediate quantities (dish label, grams, confidence, per-item calories) and suppresses strong dish assertions when classifier confidence is below threshold.

\subsection{Analysis}
The most reliable quantitative gain is phase-4 improvement over phase 3 on the matched split. Phase 6 remains useful for interpretability because users can inspect predicted grams and explicit nutrient conversion.

Largest errors appear in mixed dishes, strong occlusion/viewpoint changes, and foods with similar geometry but different density. In deployment, uncertainty is reported as $\mu\pm\sigma$ and $\mu\pm2\sigma$ to support safer interpretation (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}).

\section{Conclusions}
This team project produced an end-to-end nutrition estimation system that is practical and extensible. The integrated pipeline combines segmentation, depth, lightweight regressors, uncertainty reporting, and food-prior retrieval in a real-time Gradio demo. Future work will expand dish coverage and improve calibration between classifier confidence and nutrient priors.
