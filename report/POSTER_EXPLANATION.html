1. Problem Statement & Motivation

The Core Challenge

Nutritionists and fitness enthusiasts need accurate calorie and macronutrient estimates for food. Traditionally, this requires:

  • Food weighing: Precise kitchen scales (cumbersome for daily meals)
  • Manual lookup: USDA database searches (time-consuming)
  • Eyeballing portions: Error-prone (±50% uncertainty typical)

Your Innovation: Estimate nutrition from a single RGB photograph using computer vision + machine learning, with quantified uncertainty.

Why Visual Appearance Alone Isn't Enough

Example: A bowl of pasta looks identical whether it weighs 200g (dry weight) or 500g (cooked weight). The cooked version has ~150% more calories. Visual features alone cannot distinguish between them.

Your Solution: Combine three information sources:

  • RGB texture: Learn food type patterns (e.g., tomato sauce is red)
  • Segmentation mask: Determine the boundary of the food item
  • Depth map: Estimate 3D structure, volume, and mass

2. Evaluation Metrics: MAE, RMSE, MAPE

Why Multiple Metrics?

Different metrics capture different failure modes. You use three:

MAE (Mean Absolute Error)
$$\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left| y_i - \hat{y}_i \right|$$

What it means: Average prediction error in the same units as the target (kcal, grams, etc.)

Example: MAE = 119.79 kcal means on average your prediction is off by ±120 calories.

Why use it: Intuitive, robust to outliers (doesn't penalize extreme errors as harshly).

When it matters: For user-facing apps, MAE feels fair—big errors count the same as small ones.

RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)
$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - \hat{y}_i \right)^2}$$

What it means: Penalizes large errors more heavily than small ones.

Example: RMSE = 158.69 kcal vs MAE = 119.79 kcal. The gap (39 kcal) indicates you have some larger prediction errors.

Why use it: Encourages the model to avoid extreme failures.

When it matters: Safety-critical applications. RMSE > MAE signals high variance.

MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)
$$\text{MAPE} = \frac{100\%}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left| \frac{y_i - \hat{y}_i}{y_i} \right|$$

What it means: Relative error (percentage). Independent of scale.

Example: Predicting 100 kcal when actual is 200 kcal = 50% error.

Why you use it secondarily: Can be unstable when true values are small (division by near-zero).

Rank Metric Reason
Primary MAE & RMSE Stable, actionable in real units (kcal, grams)
Secondary MAPE Provides relative perspective; less reliable for small values

3. The 9 Geometric Features (Detailed Breakdown)

Definition: A 9-dimensional vector g = [f₁, f₂, ..., f₉] extracted from the food mask and depth map. These replace raw pixels as input to the nutrition MLP.

Why Geometry Instead of Pixels?

  • Scale invariance: A 100g meatball and a 500g meatball look similar in RGB; geometry captures the difference.
  • Physical grounding: Calories correlate with mass/volume, not appearance.
  • Dimensionality reduction: From 1.2M pixels → 9 numbers; easier to learn from limited data.

The 9 Features (Exact Definitions)

1. Mask Area (pixels)

Computation: Sum of all pixels in the segmentation mask.

Formula: area = count(mask > 0.5)

Interpretation: Larger food footprint in image → likely more food mass.

Range: 1,000 – 500,000 pixels (depends on camera distance).

2. Mask Perimeter (pixels)

Computation: Boundary length of the food contour.

Method: Trace the edge of the mask, sum edge lengths.

Interpretation: Complex, convoluted foods (e.g., pasta) have high perimeter.

Signal: Shape complexity can hint at density variations.

3. Convex Hull Area (pixels)

Computation: Area of the smallest convex shape enclosing the mask.

Method: Convex hull algorithm (Graham scan).

Interpretation: Ratio of (mask area / convex hull area) = shape solidity.

Example: Soup (solid) → ratio ≈ 1; Spaghetti (complex) → ratio ≈ 0.4.

4. Mean Depth (raw units)

Computation: Average depth value within the mask.

Formula: mean_depth = mean(depth[mask > 0.5])

Interpretation: Higher mean depth = thicker/taller food item.

Why it matters: Two items with same area but different heights have different volumes.

5. Std Dev Depth (raw units)

Computation: Standard deviation of depth within the mask.

Formula: std_depth = std(depth[mask > 0.5])

Interpretation: High std dev = uneven food (e.g., chunky stew, layered cake).

Signal: Captures structural heterogeneity → can hint at ingredient mixing.

6. Min Depth (raw units)

Computation: Smallest depth value in the mask (closest point to camera).

Interpretation: Peaks/protrusions toward camera.

Example: A sandwich has low min_depth (bread peak); soup has high min_depth (flat surface).

7. Max Depth (raw units)

Computation: Largest depth value in the mask (farthest point from camera).

Interpretation: Overall food thickness/height.

Range: Larger range (max - min) = taller, more 3D food.

8. Depth Range (raw units)

Computation: depth_range = max_depth - min_depth

Interpretation: Total vertical extent of the food.

Signal: Range ≈ 0 = flat food (plate of cookies); Range >> 0 = tall food (glass of juice).

9. Solidity Index (unitless)

Computation: solidity = mask_area / convex_hull_area

Range: 0 to 1. (1 = perfect circle/square; 0.5 = highly concave).

Interpretation: Compact foods (fruit) → solidity near 1. Complex foods (pasta, salad) → low solidity.

Biological signal: Compact → dense; complex → airy or mixed.

How 9D Features Replace Raw Pixels

Input RGB image (1280×720×3 = 2.76M values)
↓
YOLOv8-seg predicts food mask (binary 1280×720 = 921.6K values)
↓
MiDaS predicts depth map (1280×720 = 921.6K values)
↓
Compute 9 geometric statistics from mask & depth
↓
9D feature vector g = [area, perimeter, conv_hull, mean_d, std_d, min_d, max_d, range_d, solidity]
↓
Feed to MLP for nutrition prediction

Advantages of This Approach

  • Explainability: You can inspect each feature; understand why prediction high/low.
  • Robustness to lighting: Geometry doesn't care if food is in shadow or bright sunlight.
  • Data efficiency: 9 features from 2.76M pixel values; trains with fewer samples.
  • Transfer learning: YOLOv8 & MiDaS are pre-trained on millions of images.

4. Mathematical Equations & Prediction Paths

The Two Nutrient Estimation Paths

Your system computes nutrition via two independent methods. Both take the 9D geometry vector as input.

Path A: Direct Nutrient Regression (Phase 4)

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = f_\theta(\mathbf{g})$$

where:
g = 9D geometric feature vector
f = MLP neural network (128 → 64 → 32 layers)
θ = learned weights and biases
n̂ = predicted nutrient vector = [ĉ, f̂, p̂, ĉₐᵣb] (4 macronutrients)

Detailed Explanation: Direct Path

Input: 9 numbers (geometry)

Layer 1 (Input → 128): h₁ = ReLU(BatchNorm(W₁g + b₁))
Maps 9D geometry to 128 hidden units. ReLU adds non-linearity (enables learning complex shapes).

Layer 2 (128 → 64): h₂ = ReLU(BatchNorm(W₂h₁ + b₂)) with Dropout(0.2)
Compresses to 64 units. Dropout randomly zeros 20% of activations during training → prevents overfitting.

Layer 3 (64 → 32): h₃ = ReLU(BatchNorm(W₃h₂ + b₃)) with Dropout(0.2)
Further compression to 32 units.

Output Layer (32 → 4): n̂ = Linear(h₃) = [W₄h₃ + b₄]
4 nutrient predictions (no activation, unbounded regression).

Training loss:

$$\mathcal{L} = \text{MAE}(\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{n}) = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{j=1}^{4} |\hat{n}_j - n_j|$$

Mean error across all 4 nutrients. Model learns to minimize prediction error on training data.

Path B: Weight-First Conversion (Phase 6)

$$\hat{w} = h_\phi(\mathbf{g})$$ $$\hat{n}_j = \hat{w} \cdot \rho_j \quad \text{for } j \in \{\text{cal, fat, prot, carb}\}$$

Detailed Explanation: Weight-First Path

Step 1: Predict Weight (using same 9D geometry)
ŵ = h_φ(g) where h_φ is another MLP (structure: 128 → 64 → 32 → 1)
Output: Single scalar = predicted weight in grams

Example: If actual food is pasta, model predicts ŵ = 180g.

Step 2: Convert Weight to Nutrients Using Constants
Once you have weight, multiply by per-gram constants (ρⱼ):

$$\hat{c} = \hat{w} \times \rho_{\text{cal}} \quad \text{(calories per gram)}$$ $$\hat{f} = \hat{w} \times \rho_{\text{fat}} \quad \text{(fat per gram)}$$ $$\hat{p} = \hat{w} \times \rho_{\text{prot}} \quad \text{(protein per gram)}$$ $$\hat{c}_{arb} = \hat{w} \times \rho_{\text{carb}} \quad \text{(carbs per gram)}$$

Example (Pasta):

  • Predicted weight: 180g
  • Pasta nutritional constant: 1.3 cal/g (dry basis)
  • Predicted calories: 180g × 1.3 = 234 kcal

Where do ρ values come from?

  • First, classify food type (Phase 5: EfficientNet → Top-5 dishes)
  • Query USDA database for average macros of that dish type
  • If available in cache: use precomputed constants
  • If not cached: query USDA API (slow, ~1s per food type)

Why Two Paths?

Path A: Direct (Phase 4)

Pros: Fast end-to-end, learns implicit relationships.

Cons: "Black box"—hard to debug why prediction high/low.

Path B: Weight-First (Phase 6)

Pros: Interpretable (humans understand weight → calories). Uses external priors.

Cons: Two stages of error; errors accumulate.

Your strategy: Compute both, report both, let user choose (or ensemble them).

7. Uncertainty Quantification (MC Dropout)

Why Report Uncertainty?

Don't just give a point estimate ("200 kcal"). Users need to know confidence ("200 ± 50 kcal" = 68% confident in range).

MC Dropout Explained

Core idea: During inference, keep dropout enabled (normally it's only during training). Run the model T times with different random dropout patterns, then aggregate results.

For each forward pass t ∈ {1, ..., T}:
$$\hat{y}^{(t)} = f_\theta(\mathbf{g}, \text{dropout enabled})$$

Compute empirical mean and variance:
$$\mu = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \hat{y}^{(t)}$$ $$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} (\hat{y}^{(t)} - \mu)^2$$

Your Settings

App inference: T = 30 samples (balance speed vs. accuracy)
Notebook evaluation: T = 50 samples (more thorough analysis)
Reported intervals: μ ± σ (68% confidence) and μ ± 2σ (95% confidence)

Example Output

User uploads photo of pasta. Your system outputs:

Predicted Calories: 250 kcal
68% Confidence: 220–280 kcal (μ ± σ)
95% Confidence: 190–310 kcal (μ ± 2σ)
Model uncertainty (std): 30 kcal

Interpretation for user: "Most likely 250 kcal, but could be anywhere from 190–310 kcal. That pasta is noisy to predict."

Why MC Dropout Works

Different dropout masks → network takes slightly different paths → outputs vary → variation ≈ model uncertainty.

If all T samples give similar output, uncertainty is low (model confident). If samples vary widely, uncertainty is high (model unsure).

Low-Confidence Suppression

Safety feature: If σ is very large (high uncertainty), you can optionally suppress the prediction and flag it for manual review instead.

Example: "Unable to confidently predict (high uncertainty). Please verify or edit manually."

9. Error Patterns & Limitations

When Does the System Fail?

Error Pattern 1: Mixed Dishes with Overlapping Components

Example: Salad with various vegetables, dressing, cheese.

Problem: YOLOv8-seg treats entire salad as one mask. Geometry features average over all components. Model doesn't know how much of the mask is lettuce (low cal) vs. croutons (high cal).

Failure mode: Predicts average nutrition; could be off by ±50% for individual components.

Error Pattern 2: Strong Occlusion or Unusual Viewpoints

Example: Food partially hidden behind a fork, or shot from extreme angle.

Problem: YOLOv8 mask becomes incomplete. Depth map breaks down (can't see through occluder). Geometry features extrapolate wrongly.

Failure mode: Underestimates food mass; predicts too few calories.

Error Pattern 3: Similar Geometry, Different Density

Example: A bowl of popcorn vs. a bowl of rice (both white, similar volume).

Problem: Geometry alone can't distinguish air pockets from dense grain. Popcorn is ~90% air (low cal/g), rice is ~10% air (high cal/g).

Failure mode: Predicts same calories for both; major error for popcorn (50% underestimate) and rice (50% overestimate).

Error Pattern 4: Extreme Aspect Ratios

Example: Thin crepe vs. thick cake (same footprint, different height).

Problem: Depth range (max - min) captures thickness, but model may struggle to learn the nonlinear relationship (thick ≠ always means more calories if it's airy).

Failure mode: Occasional mispredictions for extreme aspect ratios.

Mitigation Strategies

  • Uncertainty reporting: Flag high-uncertainty predictions for manual review.
  • User edits: Gradio UI allows users to correct predictions ("I said it's 250g but model predicted 180g").
  • Food classification: Phase 5 helps user verify ("Is this what you meant by pizza?").
  • Ensemble methods: Report both Phase 4 and Phase 6; let user choose or average.
